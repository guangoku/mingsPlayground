
## Opening

On the fifteenth day, third moon, Year of the Snake, I rose from Yangcheng's gate.
The iron bird bore me across jagged Hengduan peaks;  
through cloud and waning light I came at last to Tribhuvan.

There the dusk was warm with laughter—  
ground men calling, traders weaving,  
a thousand voices braided in the air.
First sight of foreign earth, and the heart unknotted.

A garland met me, marigold blazing gold:  
in Mexico it summons the dead,  
in Nepal it greets the living, blesses the gods.
One flower, twin destinies—  
mystery folded in its petals.

## Bhaktapur

By carriage I reached Bhaktapur,  
ancient city of brick and carved timber,  
where I would tarry until the New Year's drums awoke.

Behold the city: palaces and shrines rising tier on tier,  
temple eaves stacked skyward, each crown diminishing,  
until a final spire speared the heavens.  
Brick for flesh, black wood for bone, gold for the shining crest.  
Platforms hewn high, wide steps unfolding,  
lions and elephants holding their post.

From beams jutted hosts of spirits,  
gods in flight, carved dense as forests.  
Not as China's brackets, proud in form and frame;  
here the art lies in the chisel's hand.  
Two worlds of craft, each at the summit.

In the market, tides of people surged;  
drums thundered, horns blared.  
Before the shrines, flowers flamed,  
statues bathed in color.  
Hindu gods and Buddhist saints, side by side,  
incense streams unbroken,  
ancient faith breathing still in the present hour.
